The History of the "Maltese Falcon" Statuette

The "Maltese Falcon" statuette is a black figurine of a falcon, made of lead and covered with black enamel. It was created by the artist Fred Sexton and used as a prop in the 1941 film "The Maltese Falcon," directed by John Huston and starring Humphrey Bogart as private detective Sam Spade.

The statuette was a central plot device in the movie, as it represented the "dingus," a valuable and mysterious object sought by several characters. The prop was made in two versions, one made of plaster for the lightweight scenes, and one made of lead for the heavier scenes.

After the movie was released, the statuette became a sought-after item, and several replicas were made. However, the original statuette was lost for several years until it was rediscovered in 1988 by a private detective named William J. Maloney. The statuette was in poor condition, and its black enamel had worn off, revealing its lead core.

The statuette was restored by Warner Bros. and displayed in the Warner Bros. Museum in Burbank, California, until it was sold at an auction in 2013 for $4.1 million.

Why is the "Maltese Falcon" Statuette so Valuable?

The "Maltese Falcon" statuette is so valuable because of its cultural and historical significance, as well as its rarity and uniqueness. The statuette is a symbol of the film noir genre, which was popular in the 1940s and 1950s and depicted a dark and cynical view of life. The movie "The Maltese Falcon" is considered a classic of the genre and has influenced many subsequent movies and TV shows.

Furthermore, the statuette is one of the most iconic and recognizable props in movie history, and it represents the essence of the movie's plot and characters. The falcon figurine is a mysterious and elusive object, coveted by many, and it serves as a metaphor for the human greed and desire for power.

Finally, the "Maltese Falcon" statuette is rare and unique, as it is the only surviving original prop from the movie. The other props and sets were destroyed or lost, and the replicas made after the movie were not as valuable or significant.
